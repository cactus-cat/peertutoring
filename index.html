Bridging 10 Peer Tutoring Project

Shrivaas Kaushik · Bridging 10 · Ms. Lally

My Introduction to the Course

When I first came to the class, I was putting significant effort into trying to grasp the structure of my tasks. I was so focused on my responsibilities, that I forgot to acquaint myself with the students. Although I was greeted with many foreign faces upon arrival, a few beacons of familiarity stood out to me. I knew some of the students through Jazz Band, and one of them was even the brother of my friend. I stuck close to these select individuals initially, slowly branching out and interacting with my other peers. The first couple weeks were filled with uncertainty and conscious effort, but each day was growing steadily more comfortable.

My Successes

My initial successes were based on my mathematical abilities; they helped me cement myself as a trustworthy and knowledgeable source in the classroom and made students more willing to ask for my help. Later in the course, my successes switched from purely conceptual to practical as I became more accustomed to different children’s learning styles and got to know them personally. The longer I was able to interact with them, the more successful I was becoming at making them understand complex topics. However, I would say that my biggest success was not related to the course material at all—it was the fact that students felt comfortable enough to ask for my advice on personal matters and even talked to me as one of their friends rather than simply a tutor.

My Challenges

One of my biggest challenges was trying to help students who were too shy to ask. Originally, I let the students carry on because I didn’t know them very well, but later on, they saw me as a familiar and helpful face, so I offered to look through the work of students who I knew were struggling. They always appreciated the offer and allowed me to check their answers. If I found any errors, I worked through the problem with them. Another challenge I had was keeping students on track. My blossoming friendship with the students was accompanied by amicable chats. While I enjoyed our conversations, it could sometimes take away from the student’s productivity. To combat this, I chatted with the students for a minute or so and then politely excused myself by saying that I needed to see if anybody else needed help.

Multiple Intelligences and Learning Styles

Thinking about multiple intelligences and different learning styles helped me keep the tutoring process grounded and practical. Instead of assuming there was one right way to explain a concept, I stayed aware that people process information differently, so I tried to offer a few different approaches and pay attention to what seemed to make sense. That mindset made it easier to stay flexible, to notice when something was not clicking, and to adjust without overcomplicating the session. It felt more realistic and honest, and it made the whole experience feel less like a scripted method and more like working with the person in front of me.

My Plans

I plan to attend the University of British Columbia and become a software developer. This has been my goal for a long time, but my experience as a peer tutor has made me interested in pursuing teaching as well. I learned many valuable lessons and virtues during my time in the peer tutoring class. I learned how to get to know people more efficiently and thoroughly, and this will help me with networking and making friends at university. I also improved my leadership skills and strengthened my basic mathematical skills by teaching others. The careers aspect of the class was also incredibly useful to me as I helped people practice writing their resumes and thereby reinforced my own.

My Advice

  • Try to get to know the student so that you can tailor your tutoring to their needs.
  • Be patient and understanding, especially when students are struggling.
  • Use a variety of teaching methods to cater to different learning styles.
